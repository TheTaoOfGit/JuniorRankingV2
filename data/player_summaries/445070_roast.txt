<p>The rain was coming down hard on the badminton courts that night when I got the call about Chyanne Huang. Born in 2008, she was just a kid really, but in this racket sport business, age ain't nothing but a number on a losing scorecard. The case file was thin—thinner than the strings on a cheap racquet—but the evidence was clear: 0 wins, 8 losses, and a career so brief you'd need a magnifying glass to find it.</p>

<p>I started following the breadcrumbs, and they all led back to the same dark alleys: SoCal ORC tournaments. She'd been there in 2023, walked away with two losses and a participation ribbon that probably cost more than her gas money. But did she learn? Did she stay away from the scene of the crime? Hell no. She came back for more punishment in 2024 and 2025, collecting losses like some people collect stamps, except less valuable and more embarrassing.</p>

<p>The witnesses were scattered—Allie Lau Woo, Sanchita Devnath, Weirui Yu—each one partnered with her for exactly one match before mysteriously disappearing into the doubles rotation witness protection program. Smart dames, every one of them. They knew when to fold 'em and find new partners who could actually keep the shuttlecock in bounds.</p>

<p>But here's the twist in this tale that'd make even Raymond Chandler do a double-take: despite carrying around a perfect 0.000 win percentage like a badge of dishonor, this kid somehow climbed from #88 to #57 in the rankings. In any other sport, that'd be called improvement. In badminton, that's what we call "everyone else got worse." The case of Chyanne Huang remains open, filed somewhere between "hopeful" and "somebody's gotta lose to the winners."</p>
