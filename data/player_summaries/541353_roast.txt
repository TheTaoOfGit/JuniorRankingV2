<p>Ah yes, the 2025 Richey Chen vintage - a promising young pressing from the Dave Freeman Jr. SoCal terroir, though regrettably this particular bottle appears to have been opened far too early. The nose presents immediate notes of fresh optimism, with subtle hints of "mom packed extra snacks for the tournament," though these are quickly overpowered by the pungent aroma of 75% defeat.</p>

<p>On the palate, this offering displays a surprisingly light body - ranking #124 in boys doubles and #115 in singles within the under-11 classification suggests a delicate constitution that hasn't yet developed the full-bodied complexity one hopes for in a mature competitor. The partnership with Aaron Thomas Nitish adds an interesting tandem note, though their brief two-match collaboration reads more like a speed-dating experiment than a committed doubles vintage.</p>

<p>The mouthfeel reveals concerning structural issues: while the singular victory provides a fleeting moment of sweetness, it's immediately overwhelmed by the harsh tannins of three consecutive losses. One detects undertones of "still learning which end of the racket to hold" with a surprisingly persistent finish of "better luck next tournament, kiddo."</p>

<p>This 2017 vintage shows potential for future development, though current tastings suggest it requires several more years in the cellar before reaching proper drinking age. Best served with patience, understanding, and perhaps a juice box. Not recommended for immediate consumption by ranking committees, though collectors of "adorable early attempts" may find value in this charmingly unrefined offering.</p>
