<p>The case files landed on my desk like a shuttlecock in a hurricane—messy, confusing, and somehow still in play. Scott Lin, they called him. Born 2007, which in badminton years makes him practically ancient history. The kid had a record that read like a fever dream: 46-23 across seven tournaments, a 67% win rate that should've meant something in this city of broken dreams and plastic birdies. But here's the thing about numbers in this racket—they lie smoother than a politician's backhand.</p>

<p>The trail started promising enough. Two titles in 2021, both at the U15 level, both in boys doubles. Kid was riding high with his partner Kevin Zhou, a partnership so tight they played 23 matches together—more committed than most marriages in this town. But success is like a good drop shot: beautiful to watch, impossible to sustain. By 2022, Lin hit the Junior Nationals like a moth hits a bug zapper—6 wins, 6 losses, dead even. That's not a record, that's a cry for help written in tournament brackets.</p>

<p>Then there was the curious case of Vivian Shi, his mixed doubles partner for 14 matches. In this business, you learn to read between the lines of a partnership. Fourteen matches means either undeniable chemistry or stubborn denial—and given Lin's current ranking status of "unranked," I'm betting it wasn't the former. The kid peaked at #118 in boys doubles U17, #140 in singles—numbers that whisper promises of mediocrity louder than a referee's call in an empty gymnasium.</p>

<p>By 2023, the case went cold faster than a shuttle in Minnesota winter. Last seen at South ORC with a measly 3-2 record, Lin vanished from the rankings like smoke from a chain-smoker's last cigarette. Kevin Zhou moved on, the tournaments kept rolling, and somewhere in the shadows of junior badminton, Scott Lin learned what every detective in this city knows: sometimes the biggest mystery isn't who did it—it's why they stopped trying. Case closed, file forgotten, game over.</p>
