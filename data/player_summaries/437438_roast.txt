<p>ARCHAEOLOGICAL SURVEY REPORT: Site JD-2013, Southern California Badminton Courts. Our excavation team has uncovered fascinating evidence of a junior competitor whose career appears to have been preserved in amber at approximately the U13 level. Carbon dating suggests this player, designation "Joanna Deng," emerged around 2023 with promising artifacts including a GD U11 #58 ranking, but subsequent layers reveal a civilization that may have peaked early and now exists in a state of pleasant archaeological stagnation.</p>

<p>The most significant find remains a lone championship trophy from the 2024 OBA NW OLC, discovered alongside Crystal Yao in what our team believes was a doubles partnership burial chamber. Curiously, this appears to be the only intact victory monument in the entire dig site, surrounded by the fossilized remains of four runner-up medals—three in mixed doubles with various male companions (suggesting either diplomatic alliance-building or chronic partnership abandonment) and one in girls doubles, all dating to the U13-U15 transition period when our subject apparently decided consistency was overrated.</p>

<p>Perhaps most intriguing is the Yonex U.S. Junior National Championships stratum, a thin layer of sediment containing a mere 1-6 record that our geological team describes as "catastrophic" and "the kind of rock formation that makes you question whether natural selection is still operational." The artifact density here suggests our subject may have been competing in a different sport entirely, possibly interpretive dance or competitive standing still.</p>

<p>Recent excavations reveal a curious phenomenon: while her U15 rankings show modest signs of life (#99 GS, #79 GD), the deeper we dig into U17 and U19 territory, the more the evidence suggests this civilization simply... stops existing. Her XD U19 #524 ranking appears to be less a competitive position and more a polite reminder that the badminton association hasn't forgotten she exists. Our working theory is that Joanna Deng represents a fascinating case study in temporal displacement—a player whose natural habitat appears to be permanently frozen in the early Cretaceous period of junior badminton, around age 13, when the world was simpler and opponents were shorter.</p>
