<p>★½ (out of five stars) - "Allyson Lam: The Badminton Chronicles" is a sports drama that promised so much potential but delivered the cinematic equivalent of a shuttlecock hitting the net. This 2022-2023 production follows our plucky protagonist through six tournaments with the enthusiasm of someone who clearly didn't read the script first. With a 26% success rate, it's less "Rocky" and more "Rocky Road" - bumpy, melting under pressure, and leaving audiences wondering if they should have chosen vanilla instead.</p>

<p>The film's most compelling subplot involves Lam's revolving door of doubles partners, featuring an ensemble cast including Grace y Fong, Krisha Tyagi, and Bernice Wong. Like a badminton version of "The Bachelor," each partnership promises romance but ends in heartbreak - specifically, the heartbreak of watching them lose together. The chemistry between Lam and her various co-stars feels forced, as if the casting director was just throwing darts at a board of available junior players. Michelle Li makes a brief cameo appearance for two matches, presumably before her agent found her better opportunities.</p>

<p>The movie's climactic sequence takes place at the 2022 NE NJBC OLC, where our heroine delivers a tour-de-force performance by losing all five matches without a single victory. It's a masterclass in method acting - Lam so fully commits to the role of "overwhelmed junior player" that you forget she's actually trying to win. The director clearly went for gritty realism over crowd-pleasing victories, resulting in a film that's more documentary than feel-good sports movie.</p>

<p>There is one genuinely inspiring sequence at the 2023 Midwest ORC, where Lam manages a 4-5 record - her career-best showing that provides the film's only real dramatic tension. For a brief, shining moment, it seems like she might break even, but like all good tragedies, hope is quickly dashed. The film concludes with our protagonist falling from her modest rankings (#48-#79 in various categories) to complete anonymity, which is either a bold artistic choice or an admission that even the badminton world has moved on to more promising sequels.</p>
