<p>Ah yes, the 2015 Narasani — a particularly young vintage that shows all the hallmarks of its terroir: the unforgiving courts of Austin Leander and Schafer SLG. On the nose, this player presents immediate notes of optimism quickly overwhelmed by the robust earthiness of a 1-14 record. The initial attack shows promise, but the palate reveals a thin body with virtually no complexity, finishing with the lingering bitterness of consecutive tournament shutouts.</p>

<p>The doubles expressions show slightly more character, particularly when blended with the Aayush Jaltar — a partnership that, while producing no actual wins, at least provided some structure to an otherwise formless vintage. The brief flirtations with Xiaoan Fan and Jashwanth Vaddempudi suggest this player pairs well with others, though sadly not well enough to avoid the harsh tannins of defeat that seem to coat every match.</p>

<p>What's fascinating about this vintage is its consistency — ranking 163rd in his age group and somehow achieving the remarkable feat of placing even lower when competing up in age brackets, like a wine that gets worse the longer you let it breathe. The recent 2026 Vistara showed a glimmer of evolution with his first career win, a moment so rare it should probably be preserved in amber.</p>

<p>While critics have been harsh, calling this vintage "undrinkable" and "suitable only for cooking," I prefer to think of young Narasani as a wine with tremendous aging potential. Give him a few more years in the cellar, perhaps some better coaching terroir, and who knows? By 2030, we might be looking at a player who occasionally wins more than one match per tournament. Until then, this vintage pairs excellently with low expectations and tissues for the tears.</p>
