<p>EXCAVATION REPORT #2026-03-30: Following extensive fieldwork at various SoCal and NorCal badminton sites, our archaeological team has successfully unearthed the fossilized remains of what appears to be the competitive career of one Haoyang Li (carbon dated to 2013 CE). Initial survey work began in January 2024, though our most significant findings date to more recent strata. The dig site, spanning eight distinct tournament layers, reveals a fascinating pattern of systematic defeat that has remained remarkably consistent across geological time periods.</p>

<p>Carbon analysis of match fragments reveals a player with an impressive 6-24 win-loss record, achieving a precisely calibrated 20% success rate that demonstrates remarkable consistency in underperformance. Of particular archaeological interest are the multiple 0-2 loss specimens discovered at the 2026 YONEX ARENA and DAVE FREEMAN JR. sites, suggesting this civilization had mastered the art of efficient elimination. Our team was especially intrigued by the SGVBC SOCAL CRC artifact, where evidence shows a brief 3-5 flourishing before the culture inevitably returned to its natural 0-2 state, much like ancient civilizations that briefly discovered agriculture before reverting to hunter-gathering.</p>

<p>Pottery shards indicate extensive collaboration with various doubles partners, most notably Jerry Zhengnan Mai (6 excavated matches) and Aaron Hong (4 matches), though forensic analysis suggests these partnerships were more archaeological curiosity than strategic alliance. The mixed doubles artifacts with partners Chloe Chen and Bella Liu show particularly interesting wear patterns, indicating these alliances may have been formed more out of desperation than tactical brilliance. Rankings excavated from the U15, U17, and U19 strata place our subject at #187, #331, and #475 respectively in singles—a descending numerical sequence that our mathematical anthropologists describe as "impressively linear in its mediocrity."</p>

<p>Perhaps most remarkable is the complete absence of any trophy artifacts or finals appearance relics in the archaeological record, creating what our team terms a "victory void" of stunning proportions. The site appears to have been abandoned multiple times following 0-2 defeats, only to be mysteriously reoccupied for subsequent tournaments, suggesting either remarkable optimism or a complete inability to learn from historical precedent. Our recommendation is that this site be preserved as a monument to the enduring human spirit—specifically, the spirit that keeps showing up despite overwhelming empirical evidence that perhaps badminton archaeology might not be one's calling.</p>
