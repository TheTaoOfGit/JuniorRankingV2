<p>Hanyi Zhang (born 2010) is an American junior badminton player best known for his unprecedented ability to win the same tournament twice in the same year,[citation needed] having mysteriously captured the BD U17 title at the 2025 YONEX LIBC NORTHEAST ORC on two separate occasions—a feat that quantum physicists are still struggling to explain.[citation needed] Zhang's dominance in doubles is matched only by his apparent allergy to singles success, where his #3 U17 ranking suggests he can beat most opponents but struggles when there's nobody else on his side of the court to do the actual work.[citation needed]</p>

<p>Known for his strategic approach to partner selection, Zhang has collected doubles teammates like Pokemon cards, racking up 22 matches with Yifan Liu, 21 with Ethan Wu, and 20 each with Adonia Anny Chandran and Selina Xu.[citation needed] This methodical partner rotation has led badminton analysts to theorize that Zhang operates an elaborate doubles Ponzi scheme, constantly recruiting new players to cover for his previous partnerships' inevitable collapse in tournament finals.[citation needed] His nine runner-up finishes suggest he has perfected the art of getting tantalizingly close to victory before dramatically snatching defeat from the jaws of success.</p>

<p>Zhang's mixed doubles career reads like a cautionary tale about the dangers of cross-gender cooperation, where his #13 U17 ranking represents either impressive versatility or his partners' heroic efforts to compensate for his apparent confusion when playing with someone who doesn't share his Y-chromosome.[citation needed] His lone XD title came at the 2024 YONEX NORTHWEST ORC, presumably after his partner threatened to switch sports if he didn't start returning serves properly.[citation needed]</p>

<p>Despite accumulating an impressive collection of acronym-heavy tournament titles (LIBC! NorCal! CRC!), Zhang's true legacy may be his transformation of junior badminton from a genteel sport into what researchers describe as "competitive musical chairs with rackets."[citation needed] At age 16, he has already mastered the advanced technique of winning titles at tournaments whose names sound like government agencies, proving that in the world of junior badminton, the real victory is making sense of the scheduling system.[citation needed]</p>
