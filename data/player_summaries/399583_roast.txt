<p>The name came across my desk on a cold February morning in 2025: Pratham Bansal. They said he was ranked #109 in boys doubles, #103 in singles, #94 in mixed doubles. In this business, you learn to read between the lines—and those numbers told a story darker than a Chicago alley at midnight. I'd seen his type before: the kind of player who shows up to 13 tournaments with the determination of a bloodhound and the win rate of a coin flip that's been weighted against you.</p>

<p>I started following the trail, tournament by tournament. The evidence was damning: 8 wins, 52 losses, a 13% success rate that would make a broken slot machine blush. The pattern was consistent—disturbingly so. Whether it was the 2024 Midwest OLC or the 2025 Yonex U.S. Junior Nationals, Bansal had perfected the art of the early exit like some players perfect their drop shot. Most of his tournaments ended 0-2 or 0-3, clean and efficient defeats that suggested either incredible bad luck or a talent for finding opponents who'd had their Wheaties that morning.</p>

<p>The partnerships told another story. Cali Bennett stuck with him for 10 matches—either supreme loyalty or a case of Stockholm syndrome I'd yet to crack. Shaan V Singh gave him 7 chances, Arushi Panda and Aditya Prasad Iyer each rolled the dice 5 times. These weren't fly-by-night partnerships; these were people willing to go down with the ship, repeatedly. In noir stories, that's either love or a very elaborate insurance scheme.</p>

<p>But here's the thing about Pratham Bansal that kept me up at night: the kid kept coming back. Tournament after tournament, year after year, like a boomerang with attachment issues. Most players would've taken the hint after the third 0-2 exit, but not our protagonist. He kept showing up to those Midwest tournaments like he owned stock in the company. And that, in a world full of fair-weather competitors, was either the mark of a fool or the heart of a champion. The jury's still out on which.</p>
