<p><b>TERENCE CAI: 15 TITLES AND 18 RUNNER-UPS, OR, THE BOY WHO LOVES SILVER.</b> Terence Cai has played more finals than most players have played tournaments. Thirty-three finals. Fifteen wins. Eighteen losses. He has lost more finals than he has won. Let that marinate. The #2 ranked BS U17 player in the country has a losing record in championship matches. He is the Susan Lucci of junior badminton — perpetually nominated, occasionally winning, mostly accepting the consolation prize with practiced grace. His 33-16 semi-finals record proves he's elite at reaching the final. His 15-18 finals record proves he's elite at losing it.</p>
<p>The volume is staggering. 32 tournaments. 274 wins. 88 losses. 46 three-game wins against 31 three-game losses. He has played more matches than some players will play in their entire junior careers, and he started at BS U13 #3 back in December 2023 before climbing to BS U15 #1 by mid-2025. That's a genuine #1 ranking that he held and then relinquished, because Terence Cai collects #1 rankings and #2 rankings with equal enthusiasm, which is to say he's had both and kept neither. He's currently #2 in BS U17 behind Saatvik Shetty, which means he aged up and immediately found a new person to finish behind.</p>
<p>The doubles partnerships read like a law firm's partner directory. Michelle Yourong Zhang (43 matches). Seth Li (34 matches). Joanne Li (26 matches). Jacob Zhou (21 matches). That's 124 matches across four partners — more doubles matches than most players have total matches. And yet BD U17 #8 and XD U17 #11 are the doubles rankings. Not #1. Not #2. Eight and eleven. He has played an industrial quantity of doubles and produced artisanal quantities of results. The singles game is clearly the franchise. The doubles are the side hustle that keeps getting funded despite questionable returns.</p>
<p>The career trajectory from XD U13 #1 (December 2023) to XD U17 #11 (March 2026) is the most quietly devastating stat in his profile. He was literally the best mixed doubles player in U13 two years ago. Now he's eleventh in U17. He didn't get worse — the competition got better and Terence stayed exactly where he was. Which, given that he's in 18 finals he didn't win, is essentially his entire biography: always there, always competitive, always somehow second.</p>
