<p>Emily Liu (born 2007) is a former American junior badminton player whose meteoric rise through the rankings was matched only by her spectacular descent into obscurity.[citation needed] Once ranked as high as #2 in Girls Doubles U19, Liu's current unranked status serves as a testament to either the competitive nature of junior badminton or her apparent decision that winning tournaments was "so last year."[citation needed] Her career trajectory resembles that of a shooting star: brilliant, brief, and ultimately burned out in the atmosphere of teenage athletics.[citation needed]</p>

<p>Liu's trophy cabinet, which reportedly requires industrial-grade shelving,[citation needed] houses six titles from her golden era of 2021-2023, including back-to-back victories at the 2021 SoCal ORC where she presumably ran out of opponents willing to face her.[citation needed] Her crowning achievement remains her loss in the 2023 YONEX U.S. Junior National Championships finals, a performance so memorable that it launched her into a 14-month ranking freefall from #2 to #69, eventually settling at the prestigious rank of "Who?"[citation needed] This dramatic decline has been attributed to various factors including overconfidence, underperformance, and the basic laws of gravity.[citation needed]</p>

<p>Known for her revolving door of doubles partners—including a documented 21 matches with the long-suffering Bettie Huang—Liu's partnership strategy appears to follow the "try everyone once" philosophy typically reserved for speed dating.[citation needed] Her 103-50 career record suggests she wins approximately two-thirds of her matches, which statisticians note is coincidentally the same percentage of her former ranking that has evaporated.[citation needed] Critics argue that Liu's 6W-0L perfect tournament at the 2022 SoCal Arena CRC was less about dominance and more about her opponents collectively deciding to take up chess instead.[citation needed]</p>

<p>As Liu approaches the twilight of her junior career at the advanced age of 19, badminton historians are already debating whether her legacy will be defined by her six titles or her impressive ability to disappear from rankings faster than a shuttlecock hit by Lin Dan.[citation needed] Her recent tournament appearances have become increasingly rare, leading to speculation that she may have discovered the revolutionary concept of "having a social life outside badminton."[citation needed] Whether this represents retirement or simply an extended sabbatical remains unclear, though her racket was last spotted gathering dust in what witnesses describe as "tragic but unsurprising circumstances."[citation needed]</p>
