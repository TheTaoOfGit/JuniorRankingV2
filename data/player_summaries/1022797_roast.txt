<p>Ah, the Thomas Cui vintage – a 2011 pressing that promised much in its early bottling but has developed some rather... curious notes over time. Upon first sniff, one detects hints of that promising 2023 U13 final at NE OLC, a delicate bouquet of potential that briefly fooled the palate into expecting greatness. Alas, like a wine left too long in direct sunlight, what followed was a precipitous decline from a respectable #37 ranking to the current #445 – a transformation so dramatic, one wonders if the bottles were switched entirely.</p>

<p>The body of this vintage presents as surprisingly thin for its age, with a 54-65 record offering all the robustness of a watered-down Merlot. The 45% win rate lingers on the tongue with notes of unfulfilled promise and just a whisper of that one shining moment at 2023 SOUTH OLC where he managed a respectable 7-4 showing. Yet even this brief flash of complexity is overshadowed by the harsh tannins of consecutive early exits and the bitter aftertaste of watching those U13 rankings evaporate faster than champagne left uncorked.</p>

<p>Most peculiar is this vintage's tendency to partner with various appellations – seven matches with Dwijesh Makireddi, seven with Zisheng Fan, six with Vihaan Reddy Cherlakola – suggesting either an adventurous spirit or an inability to commit to a proper blend. Even more intriguing are the four matches with Amy Zhao, adding mixed doubles notes that somehow failed to elevate the overall profile beyond "student athlete struggling to find his footing."</p>

<p>The finish, I'm afraid, is disappointingly short – much like his tournament runs these days. While this vintage shows glimpses of the terroir that once produced a U13 finalist, it has aged into something best paired with lowered expectations and perhaps a good sense of humor. One can only hope that with proper cellaring and perhaps fewer partner swaps, the 2026 bottling might develop into something more... palatable.</p>
