<p>Ah yes, the 2010 vintage Talanki—a promising young blend from the junior reserves, though I'm afraid this particular bottle hasn't quite reached maturity. Upon first pour at the 2024 YONEX circuit, one immediately detects notes of inexperience with a rather sharp finish, going 0-2 at both NorCal and South before managing just a single victory at Northwest. The bouquet suggests potential, but the palate reveals a somewhat thin body that struggles to develop complexity under pressure.</p>

<p>When paired with doubles partners William Runyan Ding and Soumik Saravanan—both fine complementary varietals in their own right—our young Talanki shows improved structure, managing respectable 4-3 showings at Northwest CRC. However, the overall vintage remains inconsistent, with that tell-tale 30% success rate leaving a rather astringent aftertaste. The tannins of his defense lack the robustness one expects, though I detect hints of promise in the subtle undertones.</p>

<p>Most curious is the 2025 expression at OBA NW OLC, where this vintage finally showed some backbone with a 3-4 performance—not quite balanced, mind you, but displaying newfound depth. The ranking progression suggests this bottle may benefit from additional cellaring, having improved from #124 to #252 in singles (though admittedly in the wrong direction). One senses the terroir of junior competition hasn't fully extracted this player's potential characteristics.</p>

<p>In conclusion, while the current Talanki lacks the full-bodied confidence of premium vintages, patient collectors may wish to reserve a case. At 15 years of age, this vintage has time to develop more sophisticated notes—though I'd recommend storing it away from the harsh light of major tournaments until it achieves proper maturation. A serviceable table wine for now, with hopes of becoming something more distinguished with proper aging.</p>
