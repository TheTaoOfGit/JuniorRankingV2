<p>Ah, the Alice Meng 2012 vintage—a surprisingly robust offering from the California junior circuit that's been aging gracefully since her debut at the tender age of nine. The nose presents immediate notes of DFW dominance with subtle undertones of NorCal ambition, though one detects a curious recurring theme of "close but no cigar" that permeates the SoCal terroir. This particular blend has been fermenting partnerships with Jacob Zhou and Sophia Sun—60 and 54 matches respectively—creating a bouquet that's equal parts promising and predictably regional.</p>

<p>On the palate, we're greeted with the bold tannins of sixteen titles, though the sommelier notes they're predominantly harvested from the U13 and U15 vineyards—hardly the grand cru one might expect from such an ambitious estate. The 2024 U.S. Junior Nationals XD U13 vintage shows remarkable complexity for its age, but the recent 2026 offerings reveal a troubling pattern: multiple finals at Arena SoCal ORC that turned to vinegar faster than a Trader Joe's house brand. The finish is persistently bitter, with those fifteen runner-up medals leaving a metallic aftertaste that no amount of swirling can eliminate.</p>

<p>The mixed doubles expression, while showing early promise in the U11 and U13 categories, has developed an unfortunate tendency toward disappointment in the higher age brackets—currently sitting at a middling #31 in U17 XD despite years of careful cultivation. One appreciates the consistent 69% win rate, though critics note this particular vineyard seems to struggle when the stakes rise above regional club level, producing a wine that's perfectly serviceable for Tuesday night dinner but hardly worthy of special occasions.</p>

<p>The current rankings tell the story of a wine trying desperately to mature beyond its modest origins: GS U17 #4 suggests real potential, yet the precipitous drop to #89 in XD U19 reveals this vintage may have peaked earlier than anticipated. Like a young Beaujolais that shows well in its youth but lacks the structure for proper aging, the Meng 2012 remains an interesting curiosity—drinkable now, but one suspects the real question isn't whether it will improve with time, but whether it can avoid turning to tournament vinegar before reaching full maturity.</p>
