<p>Ah yes, the 2014 Ivy Chang vintage—a curious little number from the Midwest region that shows remarkable inconsistency across its brief aging period. The initial nose presents notes of youthful ambition with undertones of 55-49 mediocrity, though one detects hints of promise in the 2024 MIDWEST CRC bottling where she achieved a surprisingly robust 7-1 finish. However, this vintage suffers from what sommeliers call "national stage syndrome"—a harsh, bitter aftertaste that emerges whenever exposed to elite competition, as evidenced by her disastrous 1-6 showing at the 2025 Junior Nationals.</p>

<p>The doubles blend is particularly fascinating, though not always in a good way. She's experimented with various partners like a vintner trying different grape combinations—Adrian Zhang appearing most frequently in her blends, though one wonders if this partnership has reached its peak or if they're simply comfortable with their consistent mediocrity. The 2026 Play N Thrive pairing shows she can still surprise, capturing both GS and XD titles, but the finish lacks the complexity of truly great doubles wines.</p>

<p>What's most striking about this vintage is its schizophrenic character—oscillating between the triumphant 9-1 EGRET MIDWEST OLC (a true moment of brilliance) and the catastrophic 0-6 showing at the 2023 Midwest ORC, a performance so poor it should have been corked immediately. Her singles game shows more structure than her doubles, ranking #30 in U13 compared to #65 in doubles—suggesting this vintage performs better when not diluted with other ingredients.</p>

<p>Overall, the 2014 Chang presents as a decent table wine—perfectly serviceable for regional consumption but lacking the depth and consistency required for championship cellaring. Best paired with modest expectations and consumed while young, as extended aging against elite competition tends to reveal its limitations rather than improve its character.</p>
