<p>★½ out of ★★★★★ — "Kishita Arora: The Early Years" is a coming-of-age sports drama that feels more like an extended deleted scene than a feature film. At just 19, our protagonist has managed to craft what can generously be described as a "minimalist" approach to professional badminton, appearing in exactly two tournaments — both confusingly titled "2024 NE CRC" as if the screenwriter got lazy and copy-pasted the same location twice.</p>

<p>The film's most compelling subplot involves Arora's partnership with Alay Shah, a four-match romantic comedy that unfortunately lacks both the romance and the comedy. Their on-court chemistry reads like a first table read where both actors are still holding their scripts, unsure of their motivation or blocking. The dramatic arc from a respectable 2-2 debut to a catastrophic 2-5 sequel suggests either character development in reverse or a director who fundamentally misunderstands narrative structure.</p>

<p>What's particularly fascinating is how the film captures Arora's unique talent for maintaining suspense — specifically, the suspense of whether she'll ever crack the top 90 in her age group. Her current "unranked" status is a bold artistic choice, like releasing a movie with no IMDb page. The brief cameo appearances at #91 in Girls Singles and #152 in Mixed Doubles feel like Easter eggs for the most dedicated fans, blink-and-you'll-miss-them moments that hint at a larger universe we're not quite ready to explore.</p>

<p>Credit where it's due: Arora commits fully to her role as the underdog, maintaining a 36% success rate with the dedication of a method actor who refuses to break character. While "Kishita Arora: The Early Years" may feel incomplete, there's something endearing about its lo-fi, indie sensibility. This is clearly a rough cut of a much larger story, and frankly, we're curious to see what the director's cut might look like with a few more tournaments in the can.</p>
