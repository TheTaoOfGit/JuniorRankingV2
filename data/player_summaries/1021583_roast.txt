<p>Yunqi Chen (born 2013) is an American badminton player widely regarded as the sport's most promising practitioner of the "so close you can taste it" playing style.[citation needed] Chen first gained national attention in 2022 by pioneering the revolutionary strategy of losing every single match he played, posting an impressive 0-4 record across the Northwest ORC and NorCal ORC tournaments.[citation needed] Sports analysts noted that his commitment to this approach was so thorough that he managed to avoid winning even by accident.[citation needed]</p>

<p>The 2023 season marked Chen's dramatic evolution from "guaranteed loss" to "heartbreaking near-miss specialist."[citation needed] At the NW OLC, Chen reached not one but two finals—in both Boys' Doubles U11 and Mixed Doubles U11—before remembering his true calling and promptly losing both.[citation needed] This remarkable achievement of reaching multiple finals without winning a single one has been described by coaches as "statistically impressive" and "emotionally devastating for everyone involved."[citation needed]</p>

<p>Chen's partnership portfolio reads like a romantic comedy cast list, having teamed with Ryan Chu four times (presumably until Chu realized the pattern), Kevin Yu twice (a brief but intense relationship), Emily Chan twice in mixed doubles (where his ranking of #119 suggests mixed results indeed), and Kabir Madiman once (a partnership that ended before it could disappoint anyone).[citation needed] His ability to maintain friendships despite a 44% win rate has been called "either inspiring or concerning, depending on your perspective on loyalty."[citation needed]</p>

<p>Following his breakthrough 2023 season, Chen achieved the rare distinction of going from ranked in three categories (Boys' Singles U11 #66, Boys' Doubles U11 #101, Mixed Doubles U11 #119) to completely unranked, a feat that typically requires either retirement or a restraining order from USA Badminton.[citation needed] Despite this setback, Chen continues to compete, armed with the knowledge that at age 13, he has plenty of time to perfect his signature move: getting really, really close to winning things.[citation needed]</p>
