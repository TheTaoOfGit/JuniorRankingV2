<p>Ah yes, the 2009 vintage Nishaant Anumolu - a curious blend that opens with promising notes of youthful ambition but quickly reveals the characteristic undertones of a 43% win rate. Upon first swirl at the 2021 SoCal ORC, this particular vintage went 0-for-4, displaying all the complexity of tap water and none of the finish. The early years show a wine that clearly needed more time in the cellar, as evidenced by that brutal 1-4 showing at HPBC NW ORC when the grapes were barely 12 years old.</p>

<p>The mid-palate development around 2024-2025 shows remarkable improvement, with this vintage finally finding its legs at the ABC OLC, where it surprisingly captured both singles and doubles titles in the U17 category. Like a well-aged Bordeaux that suddenly finds its voice, Nishaant paired beautifully with partner Aadhya Vinod across 13 matches, creating a doubles blend that managed to elevate beyond its individual components. However, much like a wine that peaks too early, the transition to U19 competition has been less than graceful.</p>

<p>The current expression sits at a rather modest #177 in singles and #155 in doubles - rankings that suggest this vintage has more in common with a box wine than a grand cru. The body lacks the robustness needed for elite competition, though one must appreciate the persistence shown across 24 tournaments. At the 2024 U.S. Junior Nationals, our vintage managed a respectable 3-6 record, displaying enough character to avoid complete embarrassment while still maintaining that signature Anumolu finish of "better luck next time."</p>

<p>In conclusion, this is a perfectly serviceable table wine - not one you'd serve to impress guests, but certainly drinkable enough for casual consumption. With proper aging and perhaps a change of terroir, future vintages may develop more complexity. For now, I'd recommend pairing with modest expectations and serving at tournaments where the competition isn't too stiff. Rating: 82/100 - "Shows promise but needs work."</p>
