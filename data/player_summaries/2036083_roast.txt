<p>Deep in the fluorescent-lit gymnasium of competitive junior badminton, we observe a fascinating specimen: Ben Tirtohadi, a 19-year-old male who has managed the remarkable evolutionary feat of going backwards. Once ranked as high as 46th in boys doubles U19, young Ben has now achieved the coveted status of "unranked" - a classification so exclusive that tournament directors have forgotten he exists entirely.</p>

<p>In 2021, at the SoCal ORC, we witnessed Ben in his natural habitat during what scientists now refer to as his "golden period." Like a migrating butterfly that briefly emerges in stunning colors, Ben soared to a magnificent 17-4 record, claiming his first and only title in mixed doubles U15 alongside what we can only assume was a very patient partner. This singular moment of glory would prove to be the apex of his athletic career - much like a mayfly's brief dance in the sunlight before inevitable decline.</p>

<p>The documentary evidence reveals a troubling pattern: Ben's subsequent expeditions to tournaments have resembled less a predator stalking prey and more a confused tourist who took several wrong turns. His 1-4 performance at the 2023 YONEX Northwest ORC was particularly noteworthy - a masterclass in how to travel hundreds of miles to lose four times in spectacular fashion, like a salmon swimming upstream only to discover the river has dried up.</p>

<p>Perhaps most intriguingly, Ben has formed symbiotic relationships with various doubles partners - 16 matches with the long-suffering Ethan Paw, who has witnessed firsthand Ben's transformation from promising prospect to cautionary tale. As Ben's rankings have plummeted from the respectable depths of 46th to the oceanic abyss of unranked, one can only marvel at nature's cruel sense of humor. Here is a creature who peaked at 17 and has spent the subsequent years perfecting the art of competitive hibernation.</p>
