<p>★½ out of ★★★★★ - "The Richard Liu Story: A Badminton Tragedy in Three Acts" is the kind of sports drama that makes you wonder if the director confused "underdog triumph" with "extended suffering." Opening with promise at the 2023 U.S. Junior Nationals (3W-4L), our protagonist showed flashes of potential before the screenplay took a sharp turn into what can only be described as a prolonged meditation on defeat. The supporting cast, led by frequent co-star Hardik Gowrish, does their best to elevate the material, but even the most generous viewer will struggle with the second act's brutal string of goose eggs.</p>

<p>The film's most bewildering creative choice is its ranking progression subplot, where Liu's character somehow manages to slide from U13 #59 to U15 #105 despite two years of supposed "character development." It's like watching a reverse hero's journey, where our lead discovers new and innovative ways to lose matches. The 2024 U.S. Junior Nationals sequence (0W-4L) plays like a masterclass in dramatic irony—we keep expecting the comeback that never comes, while Liu's performance suggests he's still waiting for his script notes.</p>

<p>What saves this from complete disaster is Liu's commitment to the role. There's something admirably stubborn about maintaining a 22% win rate across six tournaments—it takes real dedication to be that consistently inconsistent. His chemistry with rotating scene partners (Charlie Chen, Derek Li, and mysteriously, Kara Liu in what appears to be a gender-bending cameo) suggests a performer still searching for his ideal co-star, like a badminton version of musical chairs where the music never quite stops.</p>

<p>The cinematography of Liu's court movement has improved since the early scenes, but the final act at SoCal ORC (0W-3L) suggests the director is still working out some fundamental plot issues. While this installment lacks the satisfying arc audiences expect, there's enough raw material here for a potentially compelling sequel—assuming the writers can figure out how to make the shuttlecock actually land on the opponent's side of the net.</p>
