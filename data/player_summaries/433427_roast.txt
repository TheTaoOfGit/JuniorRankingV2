<p>Ah, the 2012 vintage Linus Chan - a remarkably consistent offering that delivers precisely what one expects from an unranked reserve. This particular specimen opens with delicate hints of disappointment, followed by robust notes of 0-2 finishes that linger beautifully on the palate. The bouquet suggests early promise from his 2022 NorCal debut, though the single-loss terroir speaks to a player still finding his legs.</p>

<p>The 2023 NorCal bottling shows remarkable improvement with a surprising 2-3 finish - the kind of unexpected complexity that makes you wonder if perhaps the winemaker accidentally added talent. Alas, this proves to be a fleeting vintage anomaly, as subsequent releases return to the familiar 0-2 profile that defines the Chan estate. The partnership with Seiji Phan adds subtle undertones of "maybe if I find the right doubles partner," though three attempts suggest this particular blend remains unrefined.</p>

<p>The crown jewel of this collection remains the 2024 U.S. Junior Nationals, where our young vintner achieved a spectacular 1-4 - truly the Dom Pérignon of participation trophies. One can taste the ambitious overreach, the youthful exuberance of believing national championships are won through hope rather than skill. It's endearing, really, like watching someone order a $200 bottle of wine and discovering it pairs perfectly with their McDonald's.</p>

<p>Critics note the consistency of the Chan portfolio - never a finals appearance to muddy the waters, never a title to complicate the narrative. At 20% career vintage, this is a player who knows his place in the cellar, aging gracefully toward mediocrity. For those seeking reliability in their tournament brackets, Linus Chan delivers the steadfast dependability of a house wine - unremarkable, predictable, and perfect for those moments when you need someone to lose in the first round.</p>
