<p>The rain was falling on the badminton courts that March evening when the Unnikrishnan case landed on my desk. Fifteen years old, thirteen tournaments deep, and a win percentage that would make a coin flip look generous at 33%. The name was Aakash, and like a shadow in the night, his ranking had been slipping through the age groups faster than a shuttlecock through a broken net.</p>

<p>I'd seen this story before - started promising as a U13 with respectable numbers, Boy's Singles ranked #61, even cracked the top 100 in doubles. But time has a way of exposing the truth, and by U17, our protagonist had drifted into the murky depths of the 200s and 300s. The evidence was damning: a pattern of 1W-3L finishes that repeated like a broken record at South ORC, NorCal ORC, and that brutal SCHAFER SLG SOUTH OLC. Even the tournaments had tongue-twister names, as if they were trying to confuse witnesses.</p>

<p>The kid had more partners than a dance marathon - Isaac Joseph for six rounds, Kyler Trice for five, then a revolving door of mixed doubles accomplices including Meghna Yeluguri and Tanvi Sree Gaddam. But partnership after partnership couldn't crack the code. The most damning evidence? That 0W-2L showing at the 2024 SOUTH OLC - didn't even manage to stumble into a single victory by accident.</p>

<p>Yet something about the 2025 SOUTH CRC caught my eye - 6W-4L, his only winning record in the files. Like finding a diamond in a coal mine, or maybe just fool's gold glinting in the darkness. The case remained open, the mystery unsolved: would young Unnikrishnan find his way out of this statistical fog, or would he remain another cautionary tale filed away in the archives of junior badminton mediocrity?</p>
