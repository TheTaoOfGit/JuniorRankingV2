<p>Archaeological Expedition Report #2026-TL-001: The Telang Site Excavation. After extensive fieldwork spanning multiple competitive strata, our research team has successfully unearthed the remarkably shallow career deposits of specimen "M. Telang" (circa 2012 CE). Carbon dating confirms this artifact emerged from the primordial badminton ooze in late 2024, making it among the youngest specimens in our junior division collection. Preliminary analysis reveals a fascinating case study in rapid archaeological erosion—initial GS U15 positioning at stratum #103 has mysteriously degraded to depths of #319, suggesting either severe environmental damage or natural decomposition of competitive minerals.</p>

<p>Excavation of the 2024 NE CRC site yielded our most substantial findings: a rare balanced deposit of 2 wins carefully preserved alongside 2 losses, indicating this early civilization briefly understood the concept of equilibrium. However, subsequent drilling at the 2025 YONEX LIBC NORTHEAST ORC site revealed only barren 0-2 sediment layers, suggesting a catastrophic collapse of whatever rudimentary winning techniques had been developing. Our geological survey indicates this site may have been struck by a meteor of superior opponents, leaving only trace elements of ambition in the soil.</p>

<p>Of particular interest to the scientific community are the fossilized partnership remains discovered throughout the dig. Trilochan Janakiraman appears twice in the archaeological record as a frequent co-excavator, while Meghna Ramakrishnan represents a single, possibly experimental collaboration. These partnership artifacts suggest early attempts at doubles evolution, though their combined win-loss ratios indicate the species had not yet developed successful hunting strategies for tournament prey.</p>

<p>Perhaps most remarkably, our analysis reveals this specimen currently occupies multiple ranking territories simultaneously—a rare phenomenon in competitive archaeology where a single player manages to be mediocre across U15, U17, and U19 age classifications. This multi-temporal existence defies conventional sporting physics and suggests we may have discovered badminton's first time-traveling underachiever. Further excavation is recommended, though our carbon dating equipment may not be sensitive enough to detect future improvement.</p>
