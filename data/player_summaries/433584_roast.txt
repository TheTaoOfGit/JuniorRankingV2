Crime: Impersonating a badminton player across 14 tournaments with a 21-30 record that's colder than a corpse in the morgue. I'd seen his type before – young punks thinking they could make it big in the racket game, but this kid's story had more holes than a shuttlecock factory after a tornado.</p>

<p>Following the paper trail led me down some dark alleys. The kid started small-time in 2022 at the NorCal ORC, went 0-for-2 like a rookie cop on his first stakeout. But something didn't add up – by 2025, he was pulling 6 wins at the U.S. Junior Nationals, the kind of performance that makes you think maybe this case ain't as cut and dried as it seemed. Then I found his partner-in-crime: Seiji Phan, riding shotgun for 12 matches. Together they were like Bonnie and Clyde, except instead of robbing banks, they were robbing themselves of easy victories.</p>

<p>The evidence kept piling up like empty coffee cups on my desk. Current ranking: #142 in Boys Singles U17, which puts him somewhere between "promising prospect" and "guy who shows up to tournaments." His doubles game sits at #168, proving that two wrongs don't always make a right, especially when one of those wrongs is named Darren. The kid's been climbing the ranks though – from #99 to #89 in singles over the past few years, moving up slower than molasses in January, but moving nonetheless.</p>

<p>As I closed the file on young Zhong, one thing became crystal clear: this wasn't a case of criminal incompetence, just a kid learning the ropes in a game tougher than a two-dollar steak. Sure, he's got no titles hanging on his wall and a win rate that wouldn't impress a slot machine, but something tells me this story ain't over. In this business, sometimes the best investigators are the ones who keep showing up, even when the evidence keeps pointing to "try harder, kid." Case closed... for now.</p>
