<p>★½ out of 5 stars. "Nicolas Dolar: The Badminton Chronicles" is the kind of plucky underdog sports drama that makes you wonder if the producers accidentally filmed a documentary about someone's little brother instead of their intended protagonist. Director Junior Circuit presents what can only be described as a masterclass in creative defeat distribution, spreading 17 losses across just 5 tournaments with the efficiency of a seasoned disappointment architect.</p>

<p>Our titular hero, armed with a 29% win rate that would make even the most optimistic statistician weep, embarks on a tour de force of Northeast badminton venues with all the commanding presence of a substitute teacher at a school assembly. The 2022 Yonex Northeast ORC stands as the film's unexpected action sequence—a thrilling 4-3 performance that feels like accidentally ordering the right coffee after months of disappointing lattes. It's the franchise's "Empire Strikes Back," if you will, though unfortunately followed by several "Phantom Menace" equivalents.</p>

<p>The supporting cast reads like a rotating door of doubles partners, with Ori Proschitsky delivering a committed 4-match performance as the long-suffering sidekick. Matthew Luo and Haoyu Cheng provide solid cameos in their 2-match arcs, while Thomas Cui and Siddhi Thorat appear briefly as mysterious figures who clearly read the script and made other plans. The chemistry between Dolar and his various co-stars suggests either great versatility or that nobody wanted to commit to a sequel.</p>

<p>The cinematography of the 2022 NE NJBC OLC sequence—a haunting 0-5 masterpiece—deserves particular mention for its bold artistic choice to eliminate victories entirely from the narrative structure. It's experimental filmmaking at its most uncompromising. While critics may debate whether this young auteur will develop into the next great champion or remain forever in the "charming regional talent" category, one thing is certain: Nicolas Dolar's badminton career is the kind of beautifully imperfect art that reminds us why we love sports in the first place, even when—especially when—the scoreboard suggests we probably shouldn't.</p>
