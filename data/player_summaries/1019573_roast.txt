<p>Kenny Luu (born 2005) is a former American badminton player best known for his remarkable ability to maintain a sub-.500 winning percentage across nine tournaments[citation needed]. Luu achieved the rare distinction of being both ranked (#44 in Boys Doubles U19, #55 in Boys Singles U19 as of December 2023) and subsequently becoming completely unranked, a feat that badminton historians compare to "climbing Mount Everest and then somehow ending up in Death Valley"[citation needed].</p>

<p>Luu's most celebrated partnership was with Daphne Huang, with whom he played an impressive 12 matches[citation needed]. Sports analysts have theorized that Huang's continued collaboration with Luu demonstrates either extraordinary loyalty or a complete misunderstanding of the concept of "cutting one's losses"[citation needed]. His other notable partnerships include Lance Fuchia (10 matches) and Jaden Jin (7 matches), though neither partnership was sufficient to prevent his eventual descent into ranking obscurity.</p>

<p>The pinnacle of Luu's career came at the 2023 YONEX U.S. Junior National Championships, where he achieved an 8-4 record[citation needed]. This performance was particularly remarkable given his previous showing at the 2021 USA Badminton U.S. Junior National Championships, where he posted a 6-11 record that one commentator described as "mathematically impressive in its consistency of disappointment"[citation needed]. His career nadir occurred at the 2022 NorCal ORC, where he managed a perfect 0-2 record, leading to speculation that he may have confused badminton with hide-and-seek.</p>

<p>Despite accumulating a 30-40 career record (43% win rate), Luu never managed to secure a tournament title or even reach a final[citation needed]. Contemporary analysts suggest that Luu's greatest contribution to badminton may be his role in making other junior players feel significantly better about their own performances[citation needed]. His current unranked status represents what badminton theorists call "the natural conclusion of gravity applied to competitive standings."</p>
