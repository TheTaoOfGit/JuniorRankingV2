<p>Archaeological Survey Report #2026-SA: Following extensive excavation at the Arunkumar site, researchers have uncovered compelling evidence of a junior badminton civilization dating back to 2023. Initial stratigraphic analysis reveals multiple layers of tournament debris, with the deepest deposits yielding a particularly harsh 0-3 extinction event at the 2023 YONEX Northeast ORC. Carbon dating of scattered shuttlecocks suggests this civilization experienced frequent upheaval, achieving an overall survival rate of just 27% across documented conflicts.</p>

<p>Most intriguing are the partnership artifacts discovered throughout the site. The archaeological record shows our subject formed numerous alliances, most notably with one "Amogh Lanka" (7 documented collaborations) and "Gowri Ashrita Potnuru" (5 joint ventures). However, pottery shards and court markings indicate these partnerships rarely resulted in territorial expansion, suggesting a nomadic culture that frequently relocated between doubles combinations in search of more hospitable winning conditions.</p>

<p>The crown jewel of our dig remains the 2024 U.S. Junior National Championships stratum, where we uncovered evidence of a catastrophic 2-6 collapse. Forensic analysis of this layer reveals our civilization attempted to compete among the continental elite, only to discover they had brought stone tools to a bronze age tournament. The geological record shows immediate retreat to regional competitions, where performance stabilized at a more sustainable 2-2 equilibrium.</p>

<p>Perhaps most remarkable is the ranking evolution documented in our core samples: from modest U11 positions in late 2023 (#82 BD, #97 BS) to the current archaeological wonder that is U13 #335 in singles. This suggests either significant population growth in youth badminton or a civilization still mastering the basic principles of shuttlecock aerodynamics. Further expeditions planned pending grant funding and improved court vision.</p>
