<p>The name's Detective Racket, and I've seen a lot of cases walk through my door. But nothing quite prepared me for the file that landed on my desk this rainy Tuesday: Nathan Wu, age 9, suspected of committing multiple felonies against the sport of badminton. The evidence was damning - a 36% win rate that hit harder than a back-alley mugging, and rankings that dropped faster than a crooked cop's alibis.</p>

<p>I started digging into Wu's alleged "career," if you could call two tournaments a career. The kid rolled into the CBA MIDWEST OLC like he owned the place, managed a respectable 2-2 split - probably beginner's luck, the kind that makes amateurs think they're hot shots. But then came February's Play N Thrive MW CRC, and that's where the wheels came off the wagon. 2 wins, 5 losses. The kind of performance that makes grown men weep into their shuttlecocks.</p>

<p>The doubles partners told a story all their own. Iris Sun stuck around for 3 matches - either she's got the patience of a saint or she lost a bet. Eeshan Ganesh and Vihaan Vignesh each gave him 2 matches before presumably filing restraining orders. Smart kids. They could see the writing on the wall, written in 363 points across every age division like a neon sign screaming "MEDIOCRITY."</p>

<p>Here's the real kicker - Wu's ranked #50 in his own age group but somehow #779 in U19. That's like being the tallest kid in kindergarten and thinking you can dunk on LeBron. The kid's got rankings spread across more divisions than a crime syndicate, each one a testament to the beautiful, tragic optimism of youth. No titles, no finals, just the cold hard truth of a shuttlecock hitting the net one too many times.</p>

<p>Case closed? Not quite. See, in this business, we call kids like Wu "diamonds in the rough" - and sometimes the rough is so damn rough, you forget there might be a diamond in there somewhere. Give the kid time, Detective Racket says. After all, even Al Capone started somewhere.</p>
