<p>Ah yes, the 2012 vintage Phillip You—a precocious little number that burst onto the scene with surprising complexity for such a young grape. The nose immediately presents notes of SoCal Arena dominance, where this ambitious vintage swept both boys' singles and mixed doubles in a remarkable 5-0 display. There's a lovely oakiness from those early U11 and U13 barrels, with six championship corks to show for it, though one detects a certain... shall we say, "maturing anxiety" as the vintage ages into the more demanding U15 terroir.</p>

<p>The palate reveals fascinating dual characteristics—while the singles expression remains somewhat thin and acidic (note the precipitous drop from #22 to #23 in boys' singles), the doubles blend shows remarkable depth and structure. The Jacob Zhou pairing demonstrates excellent tannins across 24 matches, while the Felicia Ho blend offers a more delicate, nuanced profile over 23 encounters. However, recent tastings suggest this vintage may be struggling with the transition to more mature appellations—the ranking descent from BD U15 #13 to #44 indicates possible oxidation.</p>

<p>What's particularly amusing about this vintage is its tendency to reach the final tasting room only to leave sommeliers wanting—nine finals losses suggest a wine that promises much on the nose but occasionally lacks the finish to satisfy the most discerning palates. The 2025 Dave Freeman Jr. SoCal OLC represents peak confusion: winning the doubles but losing both the singles and mixed doubles finals, like a wine that can't decide if it wants to be a Bordeaux or a Burgundy.</p>

<p>Still, at 63% approval rating across 26 professional tastings, this vintage shows remarkable consistency for its age. With proper cellaring and perhaps a few more years to develop its character, the 2012 You could mature into something quite special—though one hopes it learns to close out those finals before the critics lose interest entirely.</p>
