<p>Sahana Samarthy (born 2012) is an American badminton player best known for her remarkable consistency in achieving a sub-.500 win rate across multiple age divisions[citation needed]. Despite having access to what appears to be an extensive collection of doubles partners—including the long-suffering Alex Jiang, who has endured 13 matches alongside Samarthy's particular brand of court coverage[citation needed]—she has managed to maintain a career record of 42-46, a mathematical achievement that would make even the most mediocre casino proud.</p>

<p>Samarthy's tournament career began with the inauspicious 2023 MIDWEST OLC, where she posted a flawless 0-3 record, establishing early on her commitment to keeping expectations appropriately low[citation needed]. Her breakthrough moment came at the 2026 CBA MIDWEST OLC, where she inexplicably won both Girls' Singles U15 and Girls' Doubles U15 titles, leading badminton analysts to theorize that all her opponents may have simultaneously contracted food poisoning[citation needed]. This rare display of competence temporarily boosted her rankings before gravity inevitably reasserted itself.</p>

<p>In the academic discipline of losing finals, Samarthy has demonstrated notable proficiency, accumulating three runner-up finishes including a heartbreaking Girls' Doubles U13 loss at the 2024 MIDWEST OLC[citation needed]. Her ability to consistently reach finals while possessing the court presence of someone perpetually searching for their misplaced shuttlecock has baffled sports scientists and entertained spectators in equal measure[citation needed].</p>

<p>Currently ranked #91 in Girls' Singles U15 with 2115 points—a number that coincidentally matches her approximate total steps taken during an average match[citation needed]—Samarthy continues to serve as inspiration to junior players everywhere that persistence can overcome talent, proper footwork, and basic understanding of shuttlecock trajectory[citation needed]. Her recent ranking improvement from #111 to #46 in Girls' Doubles U15 suggests either significant technical development or a concerning decline in the overall skill level of American junior badminton[citation needed].</p>
