<p>The case file landed on my desk like a shuttlecock hit by a wet noodle - soft, disappointing, and headed straight for the ground. Kai Ouyang, they called him. Age 11, born into this cruel world of feathers and strings in 2015, when hope still existed and the rankings meant something. I'd seen his type before: fresh-faced kids who thought badminton was just a backyard game until reality came knocking with a racquet-shaped wake-up call.</p>

<p>The evidence was damning. Two tournaments, eight losses, zero wins - a perfect batting average if you're going for broke. The 2024 NE CRC hit him like a freight train made of disappointment, dealing six straight defeats that would make a grown man weep. But Ouyang? He came back for more punishment at the 2025 YONEX LIBC NORTHEAST ORC, where he managed to lose only twice - progress, some might say, though I call it running out of opponents willing to embarrass a kid.</p>

<p>His doubles partnerships read like a revolving door of good Samaritans. Poor Prarthana Sanghani got dragged through three matches of whatever you'd call Ouyang's playing style, while Samuel Cheng lasted two before presumably filing for witness protection. Westin Chen was smart - one match and out, like a tourist who accidentally wandered into the wrong neighborhood.</p>

<p>The real mystery wasn't his 0-8 record or his rankings that dropped faster than his serves - it was how a kid who once cracked the top 100 in U11 singles (#95) and doubles (#73) could fall so far so fast. Age up to U13, U15, U17, and suddenly he's swimming in four-digit ranking waters deeper than the Mariana Trench. The badminton gods giveth rankings to the young, and they taketh away with compound interest when you get older and the competition stops playing with training wheels.</p>

<p>But here's the thing about our boy Kai - he keeps showing up, keeps swinging that racquet like he's got a chance, keeps believing that maybe this time the shuttlecock will listen to reason. In a sport full of prodigies who peaked before they could drive, there's something almost admirable about a kid who's professionally terrible but refuses to quit. The case of Kai Ouyang isn't closed - it's just getting started, and somewhere in those 85 points lurks the heart of a fighter who hasn't figured out he's supposed to win yet.</p>
