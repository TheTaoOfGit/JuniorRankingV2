<p>Ah yes, the 2014 Amelia Goey vintage—a delightfully ambitious young wine that's still finding its legs, much like a precocious Beaujolais that insists it belongs at the adults' table. The initial nose presents bold notes of determination with subtle undertones of "wait, what's mixed doubles again?" This particular vintage has been aged across eight distinguished tournaments, developing a complex 29% win rate that speaks to character-building more than championship-building.</p>

<p>On the palate, we detect the distinctive terroir of Pacific Northwest courts, where this vintage has spent considerable time cultivating partnerships with fellow junior varietals. The Serena Li blend shows particular promise with nine collaborative tastings, while the Shankar Namboothiri pairing offers an intriguing cross-cultural fusion that somehow managed six encounters before both parties presumably discovered better options. There's a persistent thread of resilience here—note how the 2025 U.S. Junior Nationals tasting produced a rather austere 0-6 finish, yet the spirit remained unbroken.</p>

<p>What's most remarkable about this vintage is its surprising improvement trajectory—the rankings have actually climbed from the cellar depths of #212 in mixed doubles to a more respectable #134, suggesting this wine may have been unfairly dismissed in early tastings. The 2026 Seattle Northwest CRC produced the vintage's finest moment: a singles final appearance that, while ultimately unsuccessful, showed flashes of what proper aging might accomplish.</p>

<p>The finish is refreshingly honest—no pretensions of grandeur, just solid fundamentals with hints of future greatness lurking beneath the current 11-27 record. This vintage pairs excellently with patience and perhaps a good coach. While not ready for the championship cellar just yet, the 2014 Goey shows enough promise that we recommend laying down a few bottles and checking back in a couple of seasons. Sometimes the best wines just need time to develop their full bouquet.</p>
