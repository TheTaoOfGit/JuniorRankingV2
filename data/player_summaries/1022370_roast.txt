<p>Raymond Song (born 2009) is a Canadian-American badminton player best known for his remarkable ability to reach finals and then spectacularly fail to win them[citation needed]. With an impressive seven runner-up finishes across his brief but memorable career, Song has established himself as perhaps the most consistent choker in junior badminton, a title he has yet to lose in a final[citation needed]. His specialty appears to be building hope in spectators before crushing their dreams, much like his own.</p>

<p>Song's most notable achievement is his symbiotic relationship with doubles partner Micah Wei, with whom he has contested an astounding 49 matches[citation needed]. This partnership, often described by commentators as "co-dependent" and "legally binding"[citation needed], has produced mixed results, though observers note that Wei has yet to file a restraining order. Their chemistry on court is said to rival that of a divorced couple forced to work together at a small-town hardware store[citation needed].</p>

<p>Despite ranking #49 in Boys Singles U19 with 12,315 points, Song continues to participate in tournaments with the determination of someone who has clearly never heard of the sunk cost fallacy[citation needed]. His breakthrough moment came at the 2025 SOUTH CRC, where he managed to win both XD U17 and BS U17 titles, presumably because his opponents were too confused by the tournament's acronym to focus properly[citation needed]. This victory was particularly impressive given Song's previous performance at the same venue in 2024, where he lost the BS U17 final in what witnesses described as "a masterclass in how not to close out a match"[citation needed].</p>

<p>Tournament directors across North America have reportedly begun offering Song appearance fees, not for his skill, but for his uncanny ability to guarantee dramatic final matches that keep audiences on the edge of their seats[citation needed]. His 122-79 career record represents a solid 61% win rate, though critics argue this statistic is misleading since it doesn't account for "moral victories" or "character-building losses"[citation needed].</p>
