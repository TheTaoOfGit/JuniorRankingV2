<p>Ah yes, the Potter Schafer vintage—a 2017 pressing that arrived at market perhaps a touch prematurely, displaying all the raw, unrefined characteristics one might expect from such a young varietal. Upon first sampling at the Austin Leander South OLC, this particular blend exhibited a curious 2-3 finish with notes of ambition slightly overwhelming the palate, leaving behind a lingering aftertaste of "what could have been."</p>

<p>The terroir becomes more evident when examining his partnership selections—the Darlene Axella Purwanto blend shows remarkable persistence with six shared matches, suggesting either exceptional loyalty or a concerning lack of alternatives in his cellar. The Ian Chen collaboration, while brief at four encounters, hints at a more structured approach, though both pairings ultimately produce that distinctive bitter finish that has become his signature.</p>

<p>Most intriguing is his recent Vistara South OLC performance, where this emerging vintage managed a respectable 3-4 showing—a marked improvement that suggests proper aging may yet yield dividends. The sommelier detects subtle hints of growth, with his Boys' Singles U11 ranking climbing from a rather pedestrian #120 to a more palatable #85, accompanied by a surprising 192-point complexity that speaks to untapped potential.</p>

<p>While critics might dismiss this offering as thin and lacking depth—noting the conspicuous absence of any championship corks in his collection—there remains something charmingly optimistic about this young vintage. At merely nine years of age, Potter Schafer represents the kind of speculative investment that either rewards patience handsomely or teaches valuable lessons about reading the market. Time will tell whether this particular bottle develops into something memorable or simply occupies shelf space in the vast cellar of junior badminton mediocrity.</p>
