<p><b>NOAH CHIEN: THE BOY WHO FORGOT HOW TO PLAY SINGLES.</b> XD U17 #1. BD U19 #9. BS U17 #37. Read those numbers again. Noah Chien is the number one mixed doubles player in U17 and the thirty-seventh best singles player. That's not a gap, that's a canyon. That's a player who looked at the singles court and said "no thank you, I need emotional support on the other side of the net." He has played 22 tournaments, amassed a 70% win rate, won 10 finals — and somehow still ranks #37 in singles. There are players ranked above him in BS U17 who he could beat in mixed doubles with his eyes closed. But apparently when the partner goes away, so does the talent.</p>
<p>And then there's BD U17 #88 versus BD U19 #9. Let that sink in. He is the 88th best boys' doubles player in his own age group. Eighty-eighth. He is simultaneously a top-10 player in U19 doubles and a player who would not make the top 80 at his own age level. The explanation is that he cherry-picks U19 tournaments and avoids U17 doubles like it owes him money. The strategy is transparent: play up where the points are bigger and nobody expects you to win, then avoid U17 where you'd actually have to defend a ranking. It's either genius or cowardice. Possibly both.</p>
<p>The partner carousel is something. Alexander Gu (25 matches). Rithik Rasamsetti (24). Ishi Reddy (17). Diya Shetty (15). Clarinda Jin (13). Five significant partnerships in 22 tournaments — that's a new co-pilot roughly every four events. Most players find a partner and stick with them. Noah Chien treats doubles partnerships like a Netflix subscription: try it for a month, cancel, try the next one. The Clarinda Jin pairing is the latest and arguably the best, going undefeated at the Northeast ORC. But the historical record suggests Noah will have a new partner by summer.</p>
<p>The 18-4 semi-finals record is legitimately terrifying — when he reaches the semis, he almost always wins. The 10-8 finals record is... less so. Eight finals losses means that roughly half the time Noah Chien reaches a championship match, he loses it. He is the most reliable semi-finalist and the least reliable finalist in his division. The ultimate "got there but couldn't finish" player. The good news: he's only 15 and has two more years of juniors. The bad news: at some point someone is going to ask him to play singles.</p>
