<p>Here, in the fluorescent-lit gymnasium of suburban California, we observe a most curious specimen: Henry Wang, an 11-year-old badminton player who has somehow convinced tournament directors that he belongs in the same age bracket as teenagers who can actually grow facial hair. With the confidence of a dodo bird approaching extinction, young Henry clutches his racket with the same determination his ancestors once used to navigate the Ming Dynasty—though with considerably less success, judging by his 39% win rate.</p>

<p>Watch as Henry approaches the court for yet another doubles match, having cycled through partners like a divorced reality TV star. Andrew Hu, his most frequent accomplice in mediocrity, has endured six matches of Henry's tactical brilliance, which appears to consist entirely of hitting the shuttlecock directly into the net with the precision of a storm trooper. William Lin and the magnificently-named Jonathan Alexander surya Putra have also been conscripted into this comedy of errors, though one suspects they drew the short straws at practice.</p>

<p>The most fascinating aspect of Henry's career trajectory is his ranking system, which follows the inverse pyramid of success: #81 in U13 doubles, plummeting to #482 in U19—a mathematical marvel that suggests he becomes exponentially worse as his potential opponents get older. It's as if Father Time himself is personally invested in Henry's continued humbling.</p>

<p>Most recently, at the Dave Freeman Jr. SoCal OLC, Henry managed a solitary victory among three defeats, like finding a single functioning bulb in a string of Christmas lights. Yet still he persists, this plucky young warrior, armed with nothing but 760 points in singles and an unshakeable belief that his next tournament will surely be different. In nature, we call this "optimism." In badminton, we call it "adorable."</p>
