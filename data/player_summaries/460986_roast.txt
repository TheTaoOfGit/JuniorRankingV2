<p>What if I told you that in the cutthroat world of Pacific Northwest junior badminton, there existed a player so profoundly better with a partner that his singles ranking was literally six times worse than his doubles? Meet Alvin Liu: a 14-year-old enigma who sits at #73 in U15 boys doubles but somehow plummets to #469 in U19 singles, as if the mere presence of another human being on his side of the court transforms him from recreational player to legitimate threat.</p>

<p>In twelve tournaments spanning three years, Liu has assembled a rotating cast of doubles partners that reads like a junior badminton version of "The Bachelor." Kabir Madiman got four episodes, Sean Yang managed three, while Bavish Reddi Dornala, Jimmy Qinmo Fan, and Seungwoo Lee each earned respectable two-match arcs. Yet despite this carefully curated partnership program, Liu's trophy case remains as empty as his singles game plan, with an 11-27 career record that somehow still got him to a U15 doubles final at the 2025 OBA NW OLC, where he promptly discovered that even the best wingman can't save you when the lights are brightest.</p>

<p>But this is also a story of redemption. After years of what can only be described as "character-building experiences" – including a devastating 0-4 showing at the 2024 U.S. Junior Nationals that would have broken lesser spirits – something clicked at February's Seattle NW CRC. Liu posted his career-best 4-2 record, and suddenly those rankings started moving in the right direction. His U15 singles ranking rocketed from #115 to #72, while his doubles ranking climbed from #131 to #74, proving that sometimes the longest journey is from "consistently disappointing" to "occasionally competent."</p>

<p>Today, Alvin Liu stands on the precipice of junior badminton mediocrity, having transformed his game from "actively harmful" to "cautiously optimistic." At 1,080 points in singles across three age groups and 3,557 in doubles, he's become the rare player whose very presence improves dramatically when cut in half. In a sport where perfection is measured in millimeters and milliseconds, Liu has mastered the art of being exactly twice as good with backup.</p>
