<p>★★☆☆☆ "Nivin Dass: The Origin Story" - In what can only be described as an ambitious debut from this 9-year-old auteur, young Dass attempts to craft a badminton narrative that's equal parts coming-of-age drama and slapstick comedy. The opening act at 2025's Capital NE OLC was a classic rookie mistake - a 1-2 performance that felt like watching someone try to direct their first film with a smartphone camera. The pacing was off, the execution shaky, but there were glimpses of potential buried beneath the amateur hour theatrics.</p>

<p>The real breakthrough came in February's "YONEX DFW: South ORC," where our protagonist delivered a surprisingly competent 3-2 showing that had all the hallmarks of a director finally finding his voice. Working alongside his frequent collaborator Ayden Junyi Ling - think of him as the reliable cinematographer who keeps showing up in the credits - Dass managed to elevate his doubles game to a respectable #42 ranking in the U11 division. It's the kind of partnership that works because one person knows where to point the camera while the other flails around looking for the plot.</p>

<p>However, the singles performances remain stubbornly stuck in film school territory. Ranked #65 in U11 boys' singles with a modest 277 points, Dass's solo work feels like watching an actor trying to carry an entire movie without understanding that badminton, much like cinema, is often about knowing when to let your scene partner shine. His attempts to stretch into older age categories (#227 in U13, #476 in U15) are admirable in their ambition but painful in their execution - imagine a student film trying to compete at Cannes.</p>

<p>The verdict? "Nivin Dass: The Origin Story" is rough around the edges, occasionally cringe-worthy, but shows enough promise to warrant a sequel. At 4-4 overall, he's achieved that rarest of things in junior badminton: perfect mediocrity. It's not the masterpiece we hoped for, but it's not the disaster we feared either. With more seasoning and perhaps a better understanding of when to play the supporting role, this young filmmaker might just craft something worth watching. For now, it's a Netflix-and-chill kind of career - decent enough to keep you mildly entertained, but you're probably checking your phone halfway through.</p>
