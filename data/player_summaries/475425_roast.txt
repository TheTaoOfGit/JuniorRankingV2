<p>Ah, the Franklin Cheng 2015 vintage—a curious little number that presents with all the complexity of a juice box left in the sun. Upon first inspection, this young specimen exhibits a fascinating dichotomy: robust and full-bodied in doubles play, yet disappointingly thin and acidic when left to stand alone in singles. The terroir of his partnership with Emily Feng has produced his finest expressions, yielding 26 matches of what can only be described as "adequately drinkable" badminton, though one suspects the real magic comes from the other grape in this blend.</p>

<p>The 2025 harvest proved particularly bountiful for our young vintner, producing two delightful U11 doubles titles—a LIBC Northeast and SoCal ORC that went down smooth as silk. However, like a wine that peaks too early, Master Cheng seems to have developed an unfortunate habit of choking just before the finish line, accumulating six runner-up medals with the persistence of a sommelier collecting corks. His singles game, rated a modest 63rd in U13, suggests notes of "promising potential" with undertones of "perhaps he should stick to doubles."</p>

<p>What's most amusing about this particular vintage is its ambitious aging strategy—simultaneously competing in U13, U15, U17, and U19 categories, spreading his 1375 singles points thinner than the wine at a church communion. His 85-67 career record sits at a respectable 56%, which in wine terms translates to "good enough for a Tuesday night dinner, but you wouldn't serve it to impress the in-laws."</p>

<p>The 2026 Vistara South OLC tasting revealed both his greatest triumph and most bitter disappointment—dominating the field with a 7-3 record only to lose the U13 doubles final, proving once again that this vintage, while showing promise, still lacks the fortitude for the truly important occasions. I recommend pairing this player with a strong doubles partner and perhaps a sports psychologist.</p>
