<p>It was a dark and stormy Tuesday when the Iyer case landed on my desk. The kid had all the makings of a classic cautionary tale - born in 2008, ranked #109 in singles, #188 in doubles, and sporting a win percentage that would make a casino owner weep with joy. Twenty-four wins against thirty-nine losses. In this racket, those numbers don't lie, and they sure don't flatter.</p>

<p>The evidence was damning. Take the 2023 South OLC - our protagonist waltzed in and promptly collected a pristine 0-2 record, exiting faster than a defendant with an airtight alibi. Then there was the MassBad NE OLC where he managed a pathetic 1-4 showing, proving that even in Massachusetts, some crimes against badminton go unpunished. The pattern was clear as day: Avyukt Iyer had turned losing into an art form.</p>

<p>But every detective story needs a partner, and young Iyer found his Watson in one Toshan Goswami. Eighteen matches together, and what did they have to show for it? One measly U17 title at the 2024 NE CRC. That's like solving one parking ticket and calling yourself Sherlock Holmes. The kid tried mixing up his accomplices too - Aagarshna Selvaraju, Eric Liu, Sophie Pan - but lightning refused to strike twice. Even Yuxuan Zeng got dragged into this mess for exactly one match, probably took one look at those rankings and filed for witness protection.</p>

<p>The real kicker? His ranking progression told the whole sordid tale. Started at #88 in doubles back in 2023, briefly climbed to #104, then tumbled all the way down to #188 by 2026. That's not character development, that's gravity doing its job. At the 2025 Junior Nationals, he managed a 2-4 record - barely better than flipping a coin, and significantly worse for his self-esteem. The case was closed before it even opened: Avyukt Iyer, guilty of impersonating a competitive badminton player in the first degree.</p>
