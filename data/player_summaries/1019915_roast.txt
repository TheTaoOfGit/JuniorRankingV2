<p>FIELD REPORT #2024-BDM-001: Initial excavation of the Devin Garg archaeological site has yielded fascinating insights into a curious specimen of junior badminton civilization, carbon-dated to approximately 2007 CE. Our team has unearthed twelve distinct tournament layers, revealing a 60-46 win-loss stratification that suggests this ancient civilization knew just enough about shuttlecock management to be dangerous, but not quite enough to avoid embarrassing itself at the 2022 SPBA MW ORC (4W-7L stratum).</p>

<p>Of particular interest is a pristine BD U15 championship artifact from the 2021 SoCal ORC dig site, apparently the crown jewel of this lost empire. Carbon analysis indicates this trophy was won when our subject was approximately 14 years old, which explains both its existence and why it remains the singular achievement in an otherwise barren trophy case. Subsequent geological layers show a concerning pattern of mediocrity, with the subject's ranking slowly eroding from a respectable #40 BS U17 peak to a more realistic #67-69 plateau, much like how ancient civilizations declined when they realized they peaked too early.</p>

<p>Archaeological evidence suggests our subject maintained a peculiar partnership with one "Ella Mak" across 14 documented matches, leading researchers to theorize either a) a strategic mixed doubles alliance, or b) the badminton equivalent of a therapeutic relationship where she patiently watched him develop his signature move of hitting shuttlecocks into the net. Ceramic fragments from the 2022 Junior National Championships site (13W-8L) indicate this was his Pompeii moment—a respectable showing that would forever remind him of when he almost mattered.</p>

<p>The most recent artifacts from the 2025 YONEX SOCAL ORC expedition (3W-2L) suggest this ancient badminton civilization is still clinging to life, though our carbon dating equipment keeps returning results that simply read "unranked." Further excavation is recommended, primarily to understand how someone can play 12 tournaments, win more than they lose, and still manage to achieve the archaeological rarity of complete ranking invisibility.</p>
