<p>Ah yes, the Meghana Nishant 2009 vintage - a spirited little number that burst onto the scene with remarkable early promise. The 2021 bottling was absolutely extraordinary, sweeping four consecutive GD U13 titles at HPBC NW ORC with the kind of youthful exuberance that had connoisseurs reaching for their tasting notebooks. However, much like a Beaujolais Nouveau, this vintage appears to have peaked rather dramatically in its adolescent years, leaving us with a somewhat perplexing finish.</p>

<p>The current blend presents an intriguing complexity - notes of solid doubles play (GD U19 #13) with hints of singles struggle (GS U19 #23), creating a medium-bodied experience that's neither offensive nor particularly memorable. The terroir seems heavily influenced by her partnership with one Mihir Thaha, with whom she's shared the court for an impressive 64 matches. This pairing has developed lovely chemistry, though one suspects Mihir provides much of the structure while Meghana contributes... well, let's call it "enthusiasm."</p>

<p>What's particularly fascinating about this vintage is its complete absence of finals defeats - not because of championship prowess, mind you, but rather due to an impressive ability to avoid finals altogether after her U13 glory days. It's like watching a promising Burgundy that somehow never quite develops the depth you'd expect, settling instead into comfortable mediocrity with a 55% win rate that whispers "participation trophy" rather than "podium finish."</p>

<p>The aging potential remains questionable. While there are subtle improvements in the recent rankings progression, this vintage lacks the bold tannins of true champions. Best enjoyed now with realistic expectations and perhaps paired with encouraging parents who still believe lightning might strike twice. Recommended serving: at junior tournaments where the competition isn't too stiff.</p>
