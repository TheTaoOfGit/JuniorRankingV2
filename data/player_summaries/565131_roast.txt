<p>★½ out of ★★★★★ - In the ambitious but ultimately disappointing sophomore effort "Ian Song: The Junior Years," our nine-year-old protagonist delivers a performance that can only be described as "courageously committed to the method." Director Reality serves up a coming-of-age sports drama that feels less like "The Karate Kid" and more like "The Room" with rackets, as Song stumbles through his 1-6 career with the determined confusion of someone who clearly skipped the tutorial.</p>

<p>The film's two-act structure showcases Song's versatility across multiple disciplines, though "versatility" here means ranking anywhere from a respectable #58 in boys doubles U11 to a catastrophic #1048 in mixed doubles U19 - a range so wide it suggests our hero is either playing multiple sports simultaneously or has discovered time travel. His supporting cast includes the reliable Anna Dai and Luke Gao, both of whom deserve hazard pay for their two-match collaborations with our unpredictable leading man.</p>

<p>The action sequences at the 2025 U.S. Junior National Championships provide the film's lone bright spot, where Song managed to secure his singular victory like a participation trophy made of pure determination. Unfortunately, this brief moment of triumph is immediately overshadowed by the winter disaster that was the 2026 YONEX ARENA SOCAL ORC, where he went 0-3 with the efficiency of someone speedrunning failure.</p>

<p>While Song's ranking progression shows he's at least moving in some direction (though "up" is generous), the overall narrative feels less like a sports epic and more like a very expensive home movie. The cinematography of his court coverage suggests he's still learning that badminton courts have boundaries, and his scoring consistency rivals that of a broken calculator. Still, there's something endearing about watching someone approach the sport with such fearless incompetence - it's like watching a golden retriever try to solve calculus.</p>
