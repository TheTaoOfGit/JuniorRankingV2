<p>Ah yes, the Kamalini Packiyaraj vintage—a 2009 pressing that's reached a delightfully confusing maturity at merely 17 years. This particular bottle presents with an intriguing contradiction: a full-bodied doubles performance (#11 in GD U19) paired with rather thin singles notes (#64 in GS U19), creating what we in the business call "partnership dependency syndrome." The nose immediately reveals hints of New England tournament circuits, with strong mineral undertones from those five titles at NE CRC and OLC events.</p>

<p>On the palate, this vintage delivers a robust 67% win rate with surprising complexity—104 victories beautifully balanced against 52 defeats. The 2024 NE CRC pressing was particularly exceptional, showcasing an unfiltered 11-0 perfection that left critics wondering if they'd discovered the next great vintage. However, the finish reveals some troubling notes: five finals losses that suggest this wine perhaps peaks too early in the glass, particularly evident during the 2025 Nationals tasting where it delivered a somewhat disappointing 5-6 vintage.</p>

<p>The terroir is fascinating—this wine pairs exquisitely with partners named Zeng (three different varietals tested), showing remarkable adaptability to various doubles pairings. The Yui Ching Cheung blend produced nine matches of solid harmony, while the William Zeng pairing offered an interesting mixed doubles character, though ranking #82 suggests this particular blend needs more time to develop its full potential.</p>

<p>Overall, the Packiyaraj vintage shows tremendous promise for aging—her ranking progression from #68 GD U17 to #11 GD U19 indicates this wine will only improve with time. I recommend decanting for at least one more year before expecting championship-level complexity, though the current bottle already offers more sophistication than most teenage pressings. Best enjoyed at regional tournaments where its true character can shine, rather than the sometimes overwhelming tannins of national competition.</p>
