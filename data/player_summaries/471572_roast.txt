<p>⭐⭐⭐ Tried Shrihayagrivan's during the 2025 YONEX SYNERGY NORCAL ORC and honestly? The service was almost flawless—went 15-1 like some kind of badminton Gordon Ramsay. But then I came back for the 2025 U.S. Junior Nationals expecting the same magic, and suddenly it's more like a middle school cafeteria. Eight wins, seven losses? Sir, this is supposed to be fine dining, not a coin flip restaurant where I don't know if I'm getting filet mignon or mystery meat.</p>

<p>The doubles menu is where things get really confusing. Toby Liu has been the head chef for 50 matches, and somehow they've managed to climb to #29 in U13 boys doubles—which sounds impressive until you realize that's like being the 29th best pizza place in a town with 30 restaurants. The mixed doubles section shows more promise at #17, probably because Shreya Rajesh Kumar actually knows how to season her shots properly, unlike some people who think power is the only spice in the rack.</p>

<p>What really gets me is the consistency issues. This kid went from being #7 in U11 boys singles to #5 in U13—that's like a restaurant moving from a strip mall to downtown but somehow the food got better? The six U11 titles suggest the kitchen used to run like clockwork, but those four finals losses tell a different story. It's giving "peaked too early" energy, like a soufflé that rises beautifully then collapses when you actually try to eat it.</p>

<p>Look, 113-53 overall is a solid 68% satisfaction rate, better than most chain restaurants, but when you're charging premium prices (looking at you, Junior Nationals), I expect more than "pretty good most of the time." The 2024 season was rougher than overcooked steak—going 2-2 at ABC OLC, then 2-3 at Northwest ORC? That's not fine dining, that's a food truck having an off day. At least he bounced back in 2025, but I'm still waiting to see if this place can maintain quality when the pressure's on. Reservations recommended, expectations... manageable.</p>
