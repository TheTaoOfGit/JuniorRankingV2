<p>After three years of methodical excavation at the Charlie Gao archaeological site, our team has unearthed a remarkable collection of artifacts documenting one of badminton's most consistently underwhelming careers. Carbon dating reveals the earliest stratum from 2021, when a 9-year-old specimen first emerged at the SoCal ORC, depositing a pristine 0W-2L record that would become the geological foundation for years of similar sedimentary layers.</p>

<p>The most fascinating discovery lies in the Jaylen Zhou Partnership Layer, spanning 25 matches across multiple excavation sites. This symbiotic relationship appears to have evolved as a survival mechanism, allowing Gao to achieve his career-best doubles ranking of #152 while his singles ranking fossilized at #233. Our carbon analysis suggests Zhou may have been doing most of the heavy lifting, though both specimens seem equally committed to the ancient badminton tradition of early tournament elimination.</p>

<p>The 2024 US Junior Nationals stratum represents a geological anomaly—an unprecedented 3W-6L deposit that momentarily disrupted the site's otherwise consistent pattern of 0-2W sediment layers. However, subsequent drilling at the 2025 Nationals site revealed a quick return to form with a 1W-4L specimen, confirming our hypothesis that the 2024 anomaly was merely a brief tectonic shift rather than evidence of actual improvement.</p>

<p>Perhaps most remarkable is the Perpetual Optimism Layer, evidenced by Gao's continued appearance at 24 tournament sites despite maintaining a 28% success rate that would make even the most patient archaeological expedition question its funding. The consistency of these deposits across California's tournament landscape suggests either admirable determination or a profound inability to read the geological signs. Our team recommends continued excavation, as this site promises to yield fascinating insights into the persistence of hope in the face of overwhelming statistical evidence.</p>
