<p>What if I told you that in the hallowed badminton courts of the Midwest, there lived a legend? Not the kind that wins tournaments, mind you, but the kind that makes other players feel really, really good about themselves. This is the story of Shaan Singh, a young man who took the phrase "it's not whether you win or lose, it's how you play the game" and ran with it... straight into a 5-38 career record.</p>

<p>Singh burst onto the scene in 2023 like a meteorite—if meteorites consistently crashed and burned at the Midwest OLC, going 0-6 in his debut. But champions are forged in adversity, and Shaan had more adversity than a Greek tragedy. Over three years, he would grace ten tournaments with his presence, leaving behind a trail of grateful opponents and a win percentage that made cryptocurrency investors feel optimistic.</p>

<p>The numbers don't lie: at his peak, Singh climbed to #88 in Boys Singles U19, #108 in Mixed Doubles, and #111 in Boys Doubles. These weren't just rankings—they were beacons of hope for every player ranked below him, proof that someone, somewhere, was having a worse day. His partnership with Pratham Bansal became the stuff of legend, the kind of doubles team that opposing coaches used for confidence-building exercises.</p>

<p>But perhaps Singh's greatest achievement came at the 2025 U.S. Junior National Championships, where he managed to lose all four of his matches with the grace of a swan and the efficiency of a German train schedule. Zero wins, four losses, and a legacy that whispered to every junior player in America: "At least you're not Shaan Singh." In a sport obsessed with winners, sometimes the greatest gift you can give is being consistently, reliably, magnificently not one of them.</p>
