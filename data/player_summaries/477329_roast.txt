<p>The kid walked into my office on a rain-soaked Tuesday, clutching a racket like it owed him money. Howard Sun, they called him. Ironic name for a player who seemed to cast more shadows than light on the badminton court. I'd seen his type before—4 tournaments deep, 8 wins against 10 losses, the kind of record that makes coaches reach for the whiskey bottle. His story was written in the cold, hard numbers: Boys Singles U19 ranked #323, a number that screamed "participation trophy" louder than a referee's whistle in an empty gymnasium.</p>

<p>The evidence painted a picture clearer than shuttlecock trajectories. February 2026 at the Dave Freeman Jr. tournament—his one moment of glory, going 3-0 like he'd finally figured out which end of the racket to hold. But that was just fool's gold, a brief respite before the inevitable slide back into the abyss. The Yonex SoCal tournament told the real story: 0-2, shut out cleaner than a janitor's mop bucket. The kid had fallen harder than his ranking, which somehow managed to plummet from #118 to #323 faster than you could say "overhead smash."</p>

<p>His doubles game was where things got interesting, if you could call watching a slow-motion car crash interesting. Partnered with Ziyang Wang for 7 matches, Howard had found his Watson to his Sherlock—except in this detective story, they were solving the mystery of how to lose gracefully. His Mixed Doubles ranking sat at #665, a number so deep in the basement it needed its own zip code. Poor Lisa Qiu only lasted 2 matches as his partner before presumably filing for transfer to witness protection.</p>

<p>I closed the file on Howard Sun, this teenage enigma wrapped in a riddle, served with a side of unforced errors. He was the kind of player who made you believe in moral victories, mainly because actual victories seemed about as likely as finding a decent cup of coffee in this godforsaken office. But hey, in this dark world of competitive badminton, someone's got to keep the bottom half of the rankings warm. The sun may not always shine on Howard Sun, but at least he shows up to cast his shadow.</p>
