<p>After months of painstaking excavation through the digital archives of USA Badminton, our archaeological team has uncovered the preserved remains of one Isaac Yang's junior career, buried beneath layers of forgotten ORC tournaments and partner rotations. Carbon dating places the specimen's peak activity between 2021-2023 CE, with the most significant artifacts concentrated in what historians now call the "Great Regional Circuit Domination Period." Initial surface surveys revealed an impressive 86-32 win-loss ratio, leading researchers to hypothesize they had discovered the tomb of a badminton pharaoh, only to later realize this was merely the burial site of someone who peaked at crushing teenagers in California.</p>

<p>The most fascinating archaeological evidence emerged from the 2022 strata, where Yang's fossilized tournament entries show him collecting U19 titles like a suburban mom hoarding coupons. Our team documented eleven championship trophies from this layer, including a remarkable triple-crown performance at the SPBA MW ORC where he somehow won Boys Singles twice—a feat that either demonstrates clerical incompetence or Yang's ability to clone himself for maximum regional dominance. The artifact quality degrades significantly when examining his Junior National Championships performance, where despite winning Mixed Doubles U19, he managed to lose the Boys Singles final, suggesting even archaeological specimens have their limitations when faced with actual competition.</p>

<p>Perhaps most intriguing to our research team was the discovery of Yang's extensive partnership network, with co-artifacts including frequent collaborators Angie Huang (14 matches) and Chloe Ho (12 matches). The abundance of mixed doubles partnerships in the archaeological record suggests Yang understood that success in badminton, much like in archaeology, requires knowing how to work well with others—particularly when those others can cover for your inevitable lapses in footwork. The site's youngest layers reveal rankings of #54 in Boys Singles and #79 in Boys Doubles before the specimen mysteriously vanishes from all recorded history, leading our team to conclude that Yang's career suffered the same fate as most junior badminton achievements: complete irrelevance upon aging out of youth categories.</p>
