<p>After months of painstaking excavation at the Midwest badminton sites, our archaeological team has uncovered a fascinating specimen: the remarkably brief competitive career of one Iris Wang, circa 2023 CE. Carbon dating reveals this career lasted approximately two tournaments before vanishing into the archaeological record, leaving behind only a 6-11 win-loss ratio and what appears to be a collection of participation ribbons.</p>

<p>Our stratigraphy analysis reveals two distinct cultural layers: the mysterious "OLC" civilization where Wang achieved a 3-6 record, followed by the slightly more advanced "ORC" period featuring a marginal improvement to 3-5. Pottery shards suggest she may have been searching for the perfect doubles partner, cycling through artifacts labeled "Tovi Tan," "Lucas Lu," "Qingyao Liu," and briefly, "Chelsie Cheng," like an ancient badminton speed-dating experiment.</p>

<p>Perhaps most intriguing are the ranking tablets discovered at the site, showing Wang simultaneously holding positions #63, #113, #54, and #94 across various age divisions—a remarkable feat of bureaucratic archaeology that suggests she was either extremely versatile or the ancient scoring scribes had consumed too much Gatorade. The hieroglyphics indicate she peaked at #54 in Girls Singles U11, a height from which she apparently decided retirement was the wisest career move.</p>

<p>Our research concludes that this brief but memorable civilization collapsed not from external pressures or natural disasters, but simply because someone discovered that fourth grade homework was more predictable than badminton tournaments. The site now lies dormant, though local legends speak of a possible return expedition sometime after algebra class.</p>
