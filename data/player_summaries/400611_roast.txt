<p>Archaeological Survey Report #2026-SC: Following reports of unusual seismic activity in the Pacific Northwest, our team has uncovered what appears to be the fossilized remains of a junior badminton career, carbon-dated to approximately 2023-2025 CE. The specimen, identified as "Sanchay Devnath," represents a fascinating glimpse into the extinct practice of competitive optimism. Initial excavation revealed a remarkably preserved 4-17 win-loss record, suggesting this organism may have suffered from a rare evolutionary condition known as "chronic first-round extinction syndrome."</p>

<p>Layer analysis reveals a disturbing pattern of ritualistic self-sacrifice across eight distinct tournament sites, from the 2023 YONEX U.S. Junior Nationals (where our subject achieved a perfect 0-2 elimination) to the 2025 SOCAL ORC (another flawless 0-2 performance). Particularly intriguing is the 2024 SoCal ORC & SHONAI Selection Event artifact, which shows evidence of a rare triple-loss phenomenon — a feat so remarkable that modern scientists previously believed it mathematically impossible. Carbon isotope analysis of his February 2025 rankings (#134 in doubles, #99 in singles) suggests this specimen may have actually been moving backwards through time.</p>

<p>Partnership pottery shards indicate our subject attempted symbiotic relationships with various doubles companions, most notably a three-match alliance with one "Nikhil Kandalam." However, archaeological evidence suggests these partnerships followed the same tragic pattern as ancient Titanic-era deck chair arrangements — beautifully organized right up until the inevitable sinking. The discovery of singles and doubles artifacts in the same dig site confirms this specimen was remarkably ambitious, pursuing failure across multiple disciplines with the dedication of a Renaissance master.</p>

<p>Most remarkably, this career fossil shows no signs of major tournament victories or finals appearances, making it one of the most perfectly preserved examples of "pre-success era" junior badminton we've ever encountered. The specimen appears to have been naturally mummified by its own consistency, creating an almost pristine snapshot of what competitive badminton looked like before the invention of winning. Our team recommends immediate preservation of this site — future generations deserve to witness such a complete archaeological record of the phrase "better luck next time."</p>
