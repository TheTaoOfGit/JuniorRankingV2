<p>After extensive excavation at the Om Kane badminton site, our archaeological team has uncovered fascinating evidence of a career buried beneath layers of statistical sediment. Carbon dating of tournament records reveals a 3-14 fossil record dating back to 2023, with specimens showing remarkable consistency in their preservation at the bottom of draw sheets. The most striking artifact discovered was a pristine ranking of #1090 in XD U19 – so perfectly preserved in its original state that one might assume it was recently deposited.</p>

<p>Stratigraphy analysis reveals five distinct tournament layers, each containing abundant deposits of first-round exits. The FORTIUS SOUTH CRC stratum proved particularly rich, yielding a complete 0-6 collection that our lab has determined to be 100% authentic. Notably absent from all excavated layers are any traces of championship trophies or finalist medals, leading researchers to conclude this site predates the invention of winning.</p>

<p>Perhaps most intriguing is the doubles partnership pottery shards scattered throughout the dig site. Fragments bearing the names Vidruth Arun, Avni Amol Pawar, and Aarav Juven Prabhu suggest a nomadic lifestyle, constantly seeking new partners like an ancient civilization searching for fertile badminton ground. The fact that even Vidruth ARUN appears separately in the record indicates either remarkable dedication to documentation or a player so forgettable that scorekeepers couldn't remember they'd already written his partner's name.</p>

<p>The most puzzling discovery remains the ranking progression timeline, which shows clear evidence of reverse evolution – somehow managing to get worse across multiple age categories simultaneously. This defies known laws of badminton physics and has led our team to theorize that Om Kane may have discovered a previously unknown method of time travel, allowing him to lose matches in the past to lower his current rankings. Truly, this is a career that belongs in a museum.</p>
