<p>Ah yes, the 2015 Timothy Xu vintage—a spirited young blend from the junior circuit that presents with all the complexity of a gas station Merlot. Upon first taste at the 2024 YONEX NORCAL ORC, this particular bottle displayed promising carbonation but ultimately fell flat with a 2-3 finish, suggesting perhaps the cork was pulled a bit too early in his development.</p>

<p>The most notable characteristic of this vintage is its remarkable partnership with the Devaansh Mahato varietal, creating a surprisingly robust doubles blend that achieved its singular moment of brilliance at the 2025 ABC OLC. Here, the Xu-Mahato pairing fermented beautifully into a 9-1 BD U11 championship—a brief, shining moment where all the tannins aligned and the finish was actually quite pleasant. However, much like a wine that peaks early, subsequent tastings have revealed a troubling decline in complexity.</p>

<p>What's particularly fascinating about this vintage is how it seems to lose its potency when exposed to higher altitudes—note the rather pedestrian 2-2 showing at the U.S. Junior Nationals, where the rarefied air apparently caused the entire bouquet to dissipate. The ranking progression tells a sobering tale: from a respectable #24 BD U11 to a rather astringent #88 BD U13, suggesting this particular vintage may not age as gracefully as initially hoped.</p>

<p>The current portfolio presents with rankings scattered across the palate like a confused wine flight—#223 in singles, #121 in doubles, with undertones of inconsistency that linger long after the final point. While there are hints of potential in the 59% win rate, the overall composition lacks the structured backbone necessary for premium classification. Best served immediately with low expectations and perhaps a generous pour of patience.</p>
